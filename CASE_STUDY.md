# Case Study: Nexora AI Finance Department

**How an IT Finance professional built a platform to do in seconds what used to take hours**

---

## Executive Summary

IT Finance teams are drowning in data they don't have time to analyze. Monthly close cycles, ad-hoc variance questions, contract renewal calendars, contractor burn rates, headcount planning — each area demands a subject-matter expert, and most organizations have one or two people covering all of it.

Nexora AI Finance Department is a portfolio project that solves this problem by deploying six specialized AI agents, each pre-loaded with FP&A domain expertise and grounded in real financial data. A CFO can ask "What are our biggest risks this quarter?" and receive a board-ready risk summary in under two seconds — with cited dollar figures, root cause analysis, and prioritized action items.

This case study documents the business problem, design thinking, technical implementation, and outcomes.

---

## The Problem

### IT Finance Is a High-Complexity, Low-Headcount Function

A typical IT Finance team at a mid-size company (1,000–5,000 employees) covers:

- **Budget management**: Annual plan across 15–30 cost centers, monthly close, variance commentary
- **Forecast**: Rolling 3-month and full-year reforecast, scenario analysis, management adjustments
- **External labor**: 10–30 active contractor engagements, SOW compliance, burn rate tracking
- **Vendor contracts**: 15–50 vendor relationships, renewal pipeline, concentration risk
- **Headcount**: 50–200 IT positions, open req tracking, salary budget, backfill decisions
- **Cloud spend**: AWS, Azure, GCP billing analysis, FinOps governance, commitment optimization
- **Executive reporting**: Monthly CFO packs, CIO briefings, Board materials

This scope typically falls on **1–3 FP&A analysts**. The volume is manageable only through rigid template-based reporting that trades analytical depth for throughput.

### The Hidden Cost: Repetitive Analysis

In a real IT Finance role, an analyst spends approximately:

- **40%** of time on data collection and formatting (pulling actuals, mapping to GL codes, building variance tables)
- **30%** of time on repetitive commentary (explaining the same variance drivers in monthly packs)
- **20%** of time on ad-hoc requests ("Can you pull the AWS spend by service for Q2?")
- **10%** of time on actual strategic analysis (identifying trends, recommending actions)

The ratio is inverted from what leadership needs. Business partners want insights, not formatted tables. CFOs want risk flags, not raw actuals.

### The Gap AI Can Close

Modern large language models are exceptionally good at generating structured financial commentary when given accurate data. The challenge is **grounding** — connecting LLM capability to actual financial figures without hallucination.

Existing solutions fall short:
- **Generic AI chat** (ChatGPT, Claude) doesn't know your actuals and hallucmates numbers
- **BI tools** (Tableau, Power BI) show charts but don't generate narrative
- **FP&A platforms** (Anaplan, Adaptive) are expensive, require months to implement, and still need an analyst to write commentary

**The gap**: a lightweight, domain-specific AI layer that sits on top of your financial data and generates analysis-quality output — not dashboards, not raw LLM responses, but something in between.

## The Solution

### Design Principles

Before writing a line of code, I established four constraints:

**1. Domain-correct first, impressive second.**
Every agent response needed to sound like it was written by a real FP&A analyst — not a generic AI. That means using the right vocabulary (variance drivers, SOW compliance, EDP negotiation, committed use discounts), the right structure (BU-level roll-up, period-over-period comparison, action-oriented conclusions), and the right numbers (computed from real data, not generated by the AI).

**2. Zero hallucination tolerance.**
Financial commentary that cites wrong numbers is worse than no commentary at all. The solution: all figures in every agent response are computed programmatically from a data snapshot before the response text is constructed. The LLM's job is structure and narrative, not arithmetic.

**3. Instant demonstration value.**
The project needed to work without a paid API key — for cost, reliability, and demo-ability. The mock engine needed to be impressive enough on its own that adding a real LLM was an enhancement, not a requirement.

**4. Realistic financial data.**
Mock data that looks like it came from a spreadsheet tutorial kills credibility. The dataset needed to reflect real IT Finance complexity: cost centers with non-obvious variance patterns, contractors in different status states, vendors with approaching renewals, headcount distributed across business units with realistic level mix.

### Architecture Decisions

#### The Agent-per-Domain Model

Rather than building a single general-purpose finance chatbot, I chose one specialized agent per financial domain. This was the most important architectural decision.

A CFO asking "What are our risks?" expects a risk-oriented, board-ready response. An FP&A analyst asking "What's driving the variance?" expects a drill-down into cost center and driver analysis. These are different mental models, different vocabularies, different action outputs.

By separating agents by domain, each agent can:
- Use domain-specific routing logic (knowing that "burn rate" is a contractor question, not a cloud question)
- Generate responses in the appropriate style and structure for that audience
- Surface actions that are relevant to that domain's stakeholders

#### The FinanceSnapshot Pattern

All 35+ agent response handlers receive a single pre-computed `FinanceSnapshot` object — a comprehensive data context built once at request time. This object contains:

```typescript
// A sample of what agents receive
snapshot.ytdActual          // $18,247,500 — computed from 85 actual records
snapshot.cloudVariancePct   // +8.4% — computed from 45 cloud spend records  
snapshot.overBudgetContractors  // [{name, excess, rootCause}, ...] — live data
snapshot.expiringVendors90  // [{name, daysUntil, autoRenew}, ...] — live data
snapshot.fmt                // formatCurrency() helper, bound to snapshot
```

This pattern has three benefits:
1. **No repeated computation** — aggregations run once, results shared across all handlers
2. **Consistent numbers** — every part of a response cites the same underlying value
3. **Testability** — the snapshot can be frozen in tests without mocking the data layer

#### Scored Keyword Routing

The routing engine assigns each query keyword a weight and scores all candidate routes. This beats simple `string.includes()` matching in two ways:

- **Longer, more specific phrases score higher**: "what is driving the variance" scores higher than "variance" alone, so compound queries route to the right handler
- **Negative signals cancel matches**: "cloud saving" routes to the FinOps handler even if the word "cloud" appears in other routes, because negatives suppress off-target matches

```typescript
// A route definition
{
  key: "variance-drivers",
  keywords: ["driver", "why", "cause", "reason", "explain the variance"],
  weight: 9,
  handler: (ctx) => buildVarianceDriverResponse(ctx.snapshot),
}
```

#### Conversation History Context

Each user message is stored in a conversation turn array and passed to the routing engine with subsequent messages. Two capabilities emerge:

1. **Follow-up awareness**: Short queries like "tell me more" are enriched with the previous user message, so "tell me more" after asking about cloud variance correctly routes to a cloud variance deep-dive, not a generic default
2. **Context preservation**: The agent can reference "as I mentioned earlier" and the response remains coherent across multiple turns

## The Dataset

The mock financial dataset represents a 600-person technology company with a dedicated IT organization. Numbers are realistic for this company profile.

### Design Philosophy

Real financial data has patterns that generic mock data misses:
- Cloud spend accelerates month-over-month (it never stays flat)
- Not all cost centers overspend — some run favorable, creating partial offsets
- Contractor overruns come from scope creep, not rate increases
- Open headcount generates salary savings that partially offset other overages
- Vendor renewals cluster (companies sign contracts in batches)

The dataset was designed to replicate these patterns so agent responses can reference realistic dynamics rather than artificial uniformity.

### Key Data Characteristics

**Monthly Actuals** — 17 cost centers × 5 months
- Cloud Engineering and Data & Analytics consistently over budget (strategic investment)
- Infrastructure and IT Operations consistently within or under budget (stable, well-managed)
- Applications accelerating in Q2 (ERP consulting scope expansion)

**Vendor Portfolio** — 12 contracts
- AWS: largest by spend, expiring soonest, highest negotiation urgency
- Professional services (Accenture, Deloitte): both expiring within 6 months — creates natural clustering
- Steady-state SaaS (Workday, Salesforce): auto-renewing, stable, lower risk

**Contractors** — 12 engagements
- 4 over budget — all from scope expansion, not rate increases (realistic pattern)
- 2 ending soon — creates extension decision urgency
- Distribution across 7 business units — no single department dominates

**Headcount** — 26 positions
- Critical security role (IAM Architect) vacant — creates risk narrative
- FinOps Analyst open — directly ties to cloud overspend story
- Sr. Enterprise Architect on leave — creates backfill complexity

## Technical Implementation

### Phase 1: Foundation (Data + Types + Layout)

The first phase established the project's foundation without writing any UI. Every financial interface was typed in `types/finance.ts` before any data was created. This forced clarity on what data the agents would need before the agents were built.

Key decisions made in Phase 1:
- Use TypeScript data files over JSON/CSV — enables type-safe aggregation functions alongside the data
- Compute aggregations in data files (`getOverBudgetContractors()`) rather than in components — single source of truth
- Build the layout with server components wrapping client shells — mobile sidebar state stays in a client component; data computation stays on the server

### Phase 2: Visual Layer (Components + Pages)

Phase 2 built all eight pages and the component library. The design philosophy: every visual element communicates financial meaning.

- **KPI cards** include sparklines (trend direction), progress bars (budget utilization), and delta chips (vs. prior period) — three layers of financial context in a compact card
- **Variance tables** use inline utilisation bars (hidden on small screens), two-part variance pills (dollar amount + percentage), and colored row highlighting (over/under threshold)
- **Risk alerts** display estimated dollar impact alongside severity — forces stakeholders to think in terms of financial exposure, not just operational concern
- **StatsBanner** at the top of every page provides a consistent 5-metric anchor — users always know the overall numbers regardless of which detail page they're viewing

### Phase 3: Agent Engine (AI Simulation Layer)

Phase 3 was the most technically interesting. The challenge was making the agents feel genuinely intelligent without paying per-token.

The architecture:

```
User input
    │
    ▼
buildEnrichedQuery()    ← Prepend prior context for short follow-ups
    │
    ▼
scoreRoute()            ← Score all routes for this agent by keyword weight
    │
    ▼
winner.handler(ctx)     ← Execute winning route with full FinanceSnapshot
    │
    ▼
AgentResponse           ← { answer, keyPoints, riskFlags, actions }
    │
    ▼
streamResponse()        ← Character-by-character streaming with variable speed
```

The streaming effect deserves special mention. Rather than displaying responses instantly, the chat panel streams text character-by-character with:
- 2ms delay for regular characters
- 8ms delay for newlines
- 25ms delay at sentence boundaries (`.`, `!`, `?`)
- 12ms delay at commas

This creates a realistic "AI is thinking" rhythm that makes the interaction feel genuinely intelligent. The streaming cursor (a pulsing vertical bar) reinforces the effect.

## Outcomes

**Finance Domain**
- FP&A variance analysis with root cause decomposition
- Full-year forecast modeling with scenario analysis
- Vendor negotiation strategy with leverage assessment
- Contractor burn rate and SOW compliance monitoring
- Headcount fill rate and salary budget management
- Cloud cost optimization with FinOps program design
- Board-ready narrative construction

**Engineering**
- Full-stack Next.js application with App Router

- Custom AI routing engine (no framework, no dependency)
- Responsive UI with mobile-first design
- Server components for data-heavy pages
- Character-streaming UI with realistic pacing

### What This Project Proves

1. **Finance expertise and software skill are composable.** The hardest parts of this project were the financial modeling (what should the FP&A Agent say about accruals?) and the routing logic (how do you reliably distinguish "cloud saving" from "cloud spend"?) — not the React components.

2. **Domain-specific AI is more valuable than general AI for professional contexts.** The FP&A Agent's variance driver analysis is more useful than asking ChatGPT the same question, not because it uses a better LLM, but because it knows the actual numbers and has internalized the FP&A response structure.

3. **Mock-first is a valid architecture pattern.** The entire system works without any external API. When Claude is plugged in, it inherits the routing logic, conversation history, data context, and streaming UI for free.

## Lessons Learned

**On AI product design**: The hardest problem isn't the AI — it's defining the output format. Knowing that a CFO risk response needs a board-level opener, three categorized risks with dollar impacts, and two prioritized actions is finance knowledge, not AI knowledge. The AI executes the format; the domain expert defines it.

**On TypeScript for financial data**: Typing financial data structures pays off immediately. When adding the CloudSpendRecord interface, the compiler caught three places where the existing code would have received a `number` where a `string` was expected. In financial software, that kind of error in production would be embarrassing at best and material at worst.

**On streaming UX**: The variable-speed character streaming was the single highest-impact UI addition relative to implementation cost. Three hours of work to implement; it transforms the perceived intelligence of the system.

**On portfolio projects**: The project is more impressive because it reflects genuine professional experience. The accrual checklist response, the EDP negotiation tactics, the contractor-to-FTE conversion economics — these came from doing the work, not from reading about it. No amount of prompt engineering replicates that.

## Appendix: Response Quality Examples

### FP&A Agent — Variance Driver Analysis

**Input**: "What is driving our budget variance?"

**Output excerpt**:
> "The $847K unfavorable variance is attributable to three primary drivers:
>
> **1. Cloud Engineering (+$397K)** — AWS EC2 compute scaling for the Nexora AI inference platform added approximately $76K above plan. GCP Vertex AI training runs consumed 34% more compute than the annual plan assumed. Both are tied to approved Q2 roadmap deliverables but were not granularly budgeted at the service level.
>
> **2. Data & Analytics (+$284K)** — Snowflake query costs exceeded contract minimums due to self-serve analytics adoption growing 22% faster than forecast. BigQuery slot utilization for the data lake is running above committed capacity, triggering on-demand pricing for overflow queries.
>
> **3. Applications (+$166K)** — ERP consulting (Deloitte) expanded scope in Q1 without a formal change order. SAP FICO phase 2 deliverables were added to the engagement..."

This response cites specific dollar figures, names the root causes (not just the symptoms), and distinguishes between planned and unplanned variance — the three things an FP&A analyst would be expected to do.

### Procurement Agent — Negotiation Strategy

**Input**: "How do I negotiate with AWS?"

**Output excerpt**:
> "**Our Position**: We spend $4.56M/year. We have active GCP and Azure relationships. This gives us credible multi-cloud leverage.
>
> **Target**: 3-year EDP commitment at $3.97M/year (13% discount).
>
> **Tactics**:
> - Bring GCP migration proposal to the table — AWS will defend share aggressively
> - Request minimum commitment tier pricing instead of on-demand rate card
> - Negotiate Reserved Instance credits as part of EDP package
> - Push for committed use flexibility (allow swapping instance types annually)
>
> **Walk-Away Position**: Month-to-month at current rates for 60 days while GCP migration accelerates. AWS knows this is expensive for us; they'll deal."

This is vendor negotiation strategy grounded in real contract data — the kind of advice a VP of Procurement with AWS experience would give, not a generic "negotiate from a position of strength" platitude.
